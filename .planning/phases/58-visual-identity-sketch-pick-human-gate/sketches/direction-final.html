The Colour Law

Colour is earned, never decorative.

Chrome is monochrome. A hue appears only where it carries a fact about your mail — and each hue means exactly one thing, in both themes.

  • Verdigris — confirmed. You, or another person, verified this.
  • Pencil-amber — suggested. I think so; you haven’t said yet.
  • Madder — irreversible. It earns its colour by meaning the thing cannot be undone. Nothing else may wear it — not an error, not a warning.
  • Everything else — ink. Buttons, links, nav, selected rows, focus rings. They work in weight, rule, underline and fill. Look at the States row above: none of it carries a hue, and none of it needs one.
What the discipline costs. An ink-filled button is now the heaviest object on the page — heavier than a verdigris mark. Put two of them in one pane and they will out-shout the data they sit next to. So the rule has a second half: one filled button per pane, everything else ghost or text. That constraint is real, and it is the price of keeping the colour budget for your mail. Entity type paid a smaller price: it lost its five hues and became five shapes — which turned out better, because a shape still reads in greyscale and to a colour-blind eye.
